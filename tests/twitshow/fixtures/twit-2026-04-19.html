Massachusetts' highest court ruled Friday that Meta must face a lawsuit from the state's attorney general alleging the company deliberately designed Instagram to addict children, rejecting Meta's Section 230 defense.

The ruling is significant because it distinguishes between platform liability for user-generated content—which Section 230 shields—and liability for a company's own design choices, such as notifications, autoplay, and infinite scrolling. This decision opens the door for governments to challenge how social media platforms are engineered to maximize engagement, though legal experts worry the ruling represents another erosion of Section 230 protections that could fundamentally reshape how internet companies operate. The case raises important questions about where the line should be drawn between protecting platforms from content moderation liability and holding them accountable for deliberate product design decisions that may harm users.

Section 230 Is Dying By A Thousand Workarounds, And Massachusetts Just Added Another One

"This is not a good opinion for Section 230 on several dimensions." -- Professor Eric Goldman

Massachusetts' highest court has ruled that Section 230 protections do not shield Meta from a state lawsuit alleging the company designed Instagram to be addictive to children and misled the public about platform safety, creating a dangerous legal precedent that undermines the foundational internet liability law.

This ruling represents another significant erosion of Section 230 by adopting a distinction between the "content" of user-generated posts and the "presentation" of that content—a distinction that legal experts argue is legally incoherent and provides a replicable template for plaintiffs to circumvent Section 230 protections entirely. By holding that design features like infinite scroll and algorithmic recommendations fall outside Section 230's scope because they concern "how" content is presented rather than "what" content is published, the court has handed plaintiffs a formula to attack any platform's editorial decisions. As Professor Eric Goldman warns: "If this argument works, Section 230 is dead because plaintiffs will always embrace that workaround."

7. Space

How NASA Built Artemis II’s Fault-Tolerant Computer – Communications of the ACM

Link: https://cacm.acm.org/news/how-nasa-built-artemis-iis-fault-tolerant-computer/
Date: 8-Apr-2026 7:58PM

"We can lose three FCMs in 22 seconds and still ride through safely on the last FCM." -- Nate Uitenbroek, Software Integration and Verification Lead in NASA's Orion Program at Johnson Space Center

NASA has engineered an extraordinarily redundant and fault-tolerant computer system for the Artemis II lunar mission that uses eight processors running in parallel to ensure the spacecraft can survive radiation events, hardware failures, and even total processor divergence during deep space travel.

Unlike the Apollo era's focused computer systems, Orion's architecture manages nearly all safety-critical functions from 250,000 miles away from Earth where failures are unrecoverable. The system employs a "fail-silent" design philosophy with four Flight Control Modules (each containing self-checking processor pairs), a time-triggered deterministic architecture, and a completely independent Backup Flight Software system to protect against both transient radiation-induced errors and common-mode software failures. This multi-layered redundancy approach—capable of losing three of four FCMs in 22 seconds and still operating safely—represents a significant engineering departure from modern Agile and DevOps practices, prioritizing strict architectural discipline over rapid iteration.

The Free Software Foundation has published a clarification on its position regarding OnlyOffice's addition of restrictions to the AGPLv3 license, following OnlyOffice CEO Lev Bannov's claims that the Euro-Office fork violates the license terms.

Krzysztof Siewicz of the FSF concluded that OnlyOffice has added restrictions incompatible with AGPLv3, and that recipients of the code have the right to remove them. This public clarification from the FSF addresses a dispute that had generated confusion about whether additional terms could legally be added to the AGPL license.

The FCC has granted Netgear a conditional approval to import consumer routers, cable modems, and cable gateways into the US through October 1st, 2027, despite the company continuing to manufacture these devices in Asia with no announced plan to move production to the United States.
