** Google Broke Its Promise to Me. Now ICE Has My Data. | Electronic Frontier Foundation

*** URL
https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/04/google-broke-its-promise-me-now-ice-has-my-data

*** Date
2026-04-14T16:01:48+00:00

*** Summary
"Google has received and responded to legal process from a law enforcement authority compelling the release of information related to your Google Account." -- Google (email to Amandla Thomas-Johnson)

Google handed over the data of Ph.D. student and former reporter Amandla Thomas-Johnson to Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) without notifying him, breaking a nearly decade-long promise to warn users before sharing their information with law enforcement.

Thomas-Johnson, a dual British and Trinidad and Tobago citizen on a student visa, attended a brief pro-Palestinian protest at Cornell University in September 2024, after which he fled to Canada and then Switzerland. In April 2025, ICE issued an administrative subpoena to Google for his data, and Google complied without giving him notice or an opportunity to challenge the request—despite Google's stated policy of notifying users before handing over data to law enforcement. The Electronic Frontier Foundation has filed complaints with California and New York Attorneys General, arguing that Google engaged in deceptive trade practices by breaking its promise. This case illustrates how technology companies' massive data stores, combined with government power, can enable surveillance of protected speech and create a chilling effect on free expression.

** IPv6 Usage Reaches Historic 50% Across Google Services

*** URL
https://tech.slashdot.org/story/26/04/16/1718230/ipv6-usage-reaches-historic-50-across-google-services?utm_source=rss1.0mainlinkanon&utm_medium=feed

*** Date
2026-04-19T03:18:30.536Z

*** Summary
IPv6 usage briefly reached 50% across Google services for the first time on March 28, 2026, marking a historic milestone for the internet protocol created in 1998.

This achievement is significant because IPv6 was designed to solve the address shortage problem of IPv4, which offers only about 3.7 billion usable addresses. IPv4 address exhaustion became critical starting in 2011 when IANA ran out of addresses for North America, followed by Europe in 2019 and other regions shortly after. While IPv6 adoption has faced resistance since its creation, with adoption now at 43% globally according to APNIC and 40% of traffic according to Cloudflare, reaching 50% usage on a major service provider demonstrates the protocol is finally gaining the traction necessary to sustain the modern internet's growth.

*** Summary
"Section 230 only applies to claims seeking to hold Meta liable for the harms springing directly from user-generated content they post. Meta's design decisions, by contrast, are its own responsibility." -- Electronic Privacy Information Center

Massachusetts' highest court ruled Friday that Meta must face a lawsuit from the state's attorney general alleging the company designed Instagram to addict children, rejecting Meta's Section 230 immunity defense.

The ruling distinguishes between user-generated content — which Section 230 protects platforms from liability for — and a company's own product design decisions, which are not shielded. This decision has significant implications for how Section 230 is interpreted going forward. Legal experts are concerned the ruling represents another erosion of Section 230 protections, with one lawyer warning of the "swiss cheese-ification" of the law as more workarounds proliferate, potentially undermining its structural integrity if the trend continues.

** Roblox agrees to a $12 million settlement with Nevada

*** URL

*** Date

*** Summary
"this settlement will create a safer environment for our children online, and I hope that it will serve as a bellwether for how online interactive platforms allow our state's youth to use their products." -- Nevada Attorney General Aaron Ford

Roblox has agreed to a $12 million settlement with Nevada to avoid trial, while committing to enhanced safety protections for minors including age verification systems and restricted messaging between children and adults.

The settlement addresses concerns that the gaming platform has not adequately protected children from exploitation. As part of the agreement, Roblox will donate $10 million to children's support programs over three years and spend $2.5 million on law enforcement liaison positions and online safety awareness campaigns. The company will implement rigorous safety protocols including facial age estimation technology combined with government-issued ID verification, restrict under-16 users from messaging adults unless they are "trusted friends," and expand parental controls to users up to age 16. Despite this settlement, Roblox continues to face more than 100 pending lawsuits from other states including Kentucky, Iowa, Louisiana, and Texas regarding claims of facilitating child sexual exploitation.

** FSF to OnlyOffice: You Can't Use the GNU (A)GPL to Take Software Freedom Away

*** URL
https://news.slashdot.org/story/26/04/18/0417208/fsf-to-onlyoffice-you-cant-use-the-gnu-agpl-to-take-software-freedom-away?utm_source=rss1.0mainlinkanon&utm_medium=feed

*** Date
2026-04-19T16:48:46.863Z

*** Summary
"It is possible to modify the (A)GPLv3 with additional terms, but only by adhering to the terms of the license... The (A)GPLv3 makes it clear that it permits all licensees to remove any additional terms that are 'further restrictions' under the (A)GPLv3." -- Krzysztof Siewicz, FSF licensing/compliance manager

The Free Software Foundation has sided against OnlyOffice in a licensing dispute, ruling that the company cannot impose additional restrictions on software licensed under the GNU Affero General Public License (AGPLv3) to prevent forks like Euro-Office from removing attribution requirements.

OnlyOffice suspended its partnership with Nextcloud after the company forked OnlyOffice to create Euro-Office and removed OnlyOffice's brand attribution as required by the AGPL license. The FSF's intervention clarifies that while authors can add extra terms to the AGPLv3, those terms cannot restrict freedoms already granted by the license—and licensees retain the right to remove any such "further restrictions." The ruling establishes an important precedent about what additional protections are permissible under free and open-source software licensing.

Dutch journalists successfully tracked a Dutch navy frigate by mailing a concealed Bluetooth tracker to the ship, exploiting publicly available information about how to send mail to military personnel.

** The Little Probe That Could: Why Voyager 1 Matters, and Why NASA Just Switched Part of It Off

*** URL
https://www.npr.org/2026/04/19/nx-s1-5789935/voyager-1-probe-nasa

*** Date
2026-04-19T00:00:00+00:00

*** Summary
"While shutting down a science instrument is not anybody's preference, it is the best option available." -- Kareem Badaruddin, Voyager mission manager at JPL

NASA announced this week that it has shut down one of Voyager 1's remaining science instruments to conserve power and extend the spacecraft's operational life, nearly 50 years after its launch.

Voyager 1, launched in 1977 as a five-year mission, has far exceeded expectations by operating for nearly five decades and becoming the most distant human-made object ever built, now over 15 billion miles from Earth. However, the spacecraft's power supply—a radioisotope thermoelectric generator that decays by about 4 watts yearly—is becoming critically depleted, forcing NASA engineers to make difficult decisions about which instruments to deactivate. By shutting down the Low-energy Charged Particles experiment (LECP) on April 17, mission managers hope to extend operations by roughly another year while preserving the spacecraft's most scientifically valuable remaining instruments.
